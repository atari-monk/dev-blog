It *can* definitely feel that way, especially in a world where users expect everything to be free or open-source. But while monetizing software is challenging, it's far from impossible. It just depends on a few key factors: what kind of software you're building, who it's for, how it's distributed, and what kind of value it delivers.

Here’s a breakdown of **why it feels hard** and **how people are still making it work**:

---

### 💥 Why Monetizing Software Feels So Hard

1. **User Expectations**
   Freeware, open-source tools, and freemium models have trained people to expect powerful software for nothing.

2. **Global Competition**
   You're often competing with teams across the world, some of whom may be VC-funded or willing to run at a loss.

3. **Piracy & Copycats**
   Especially for desktop and downloadable software, piracy and clones can cut into profits fast.

4. **Low Willingness to Pay (B2C)**
   Consumers are reluctant to pay for software unless it's critical to their work or life (e.g., productivity tools, gaming, etc.).

---

### 💡 How People *Do* Monetize Software

#### 1. **B2B SaaS (Software-as-a-Service)**

* Recurring revenue from businesses
* More tolerance for pricing (they pay for value & ROI)
* Examples: Notion, Salesforce, HubSpot

#### 2. **Freemium → Paid Upgrades**

* Free basic version, paid advanced features
* Works well for utilities, productivity, creative tools
* Examples: Figma, Todoist, Grammarly

#### 3. **Licensing**

* Charge per-seat or per-device (common in enterprise or specialty software)
* Also used by indie devs for desktop apps
* Example: JetBrains tools, Sublime Text

#### 4. **Marketplace Sales (App Stores)**

* One-time purchase or subscriptions via Google Play, Apple App Store, Steam, etc.
* Discoverability can help here, though platforms take a cut
* Examples: Indie games, mobile apps

#### 5. **Open Source + Paid Services**

* Give the code away, charge for support, hosting, consulting, or premium features
* Examples: Red Hat, GitLab, Elastic

#### 6. **Advertising & Data Monetization**

* Mostly viable at large scale (millions of users)
* Used heavily in consumer-facing products (e.g., news apps, games)

#### 7. **Crowdfunding & Patronage**

* Patreon, GitHub Sponsors, Kickstarter—great for niche, mission-driven, or creator-oriented software

#### 8. **Education, Courses, and Toolkits**

* Software can be a lead magnet for higher-margin offerings
* Devs create libraries or small apps that lead into paid courses or materials

---

### ✅ So, What Can You Do?

If you’re working on software and unsure how to monetize it, consider:

* **Who is it for?** (Businesses usually pay more easily than consumers)
* **What problem does it solve?** (Is it painful enough to pay for?)
* **What value does it unlock?** (Time saved? Money earned?)
* **How critical is it?** (Must-have vs nice-to-have)
